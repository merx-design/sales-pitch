## The model

This is the core of what Veren is. Before tiers, before pricing, before anything else — this is how the firm actually operates. Everything else derives from these mechanics.

### Two clocks, not one

Traditional agency engagements run on a single clock: "the project takes X months." That clock blurs two very different activities into one estimate, which is why agency projects slip.

Veren runs on two clocks:

**The Foundation clock.** Fixed duration. The design system, page builder, content model, component library, integration architecture, deployment pipeline, and 5-10 reference pages. This is creative and architectural work — finite, bounded, predictable once scoped. We commit to a Foundation timeline and absorb the risk.

**The Production clock.** Rate-based. Pages, content migration, additional templates — produced at a committed rate per sprint. The client controls velocity by funding parallel tracks or accepting sequential delivery. Ten pages or fifty pages doesn't change the Foundation cost; it changes the Production duration and cost at a known rate.

This separation is the first commercial innovation. It stops us from over-promising or under-scoping. It lets the client understand exactly what they're buying: a system built once, pages produced against it at a known rate.

### The blueprint: why it changes the economics

Our Sanity blueprint is not a template. It's a pre-built foundation layer: design system primitives, a component library with established patterns, a page builder configured for common page types, a content model covering standard needs, a deployment pipeline, and a documentation framework. It's the accumulated output of every project we've run, codified.

What this changes, economically:

**On a standard agency project:** the foundation work is 40-50% of total effort, rebuilt mostly from scratch each time. Production work (the other 50-60%) runs at the same hourly rate as foundation, because it's still developer-gated.

**On a Veren project:** the blueprint absorbs a meaningful portion of foundation work. What we build is the client-specific layer — their design expression, their specific page types, their integrations, their content. Production work runs on a different cost basis because it's primarily designer and content-ops work, not developer work.

The evidence: on our most recent Foundation-class engagement (roughly 90k EUR in value), the developer team consumed approximately 140 hours to produce 60% of the project. Even if the remaining 40% doubles that number, total developer cost on a 90k engagement is under 10% of revenue. The rest is architect time, design work, content operations, and margin.

This is not a theoretical ratio. This is what our current delivery actually looks like.

### Designer-led production: why the team shape is inverted

Traditional agency model: developer-heavy teams, because every page change requires code. Content and design are gated by developer availability, which is why agency projects have long queues and slow turnarounds.

Veren's model inverts this. Once the Foundation is in place — page builder, components, content model — pages are produced primarily by designers and content-ops, with developers re-engaging only for genuine system extensions.

The economic consequence: a project that would require 3 developers at traditional ratios can be delivered by 1 architect + 1 senior developer + 1 designer + content-ops support, at higher quality because the system is consistent by design rather than by accident.

The team composition we're building toward:

- **Architects (Victor, Merdan)** — discovery, architecture, senior review, client-facing decisions, blueprint evolution
- **Developers (currently 3, working part-time, roughly 200 hours/month total across the team)** — Foundation build, system extensions, integration work, technical implementation during production
- **Designers with system fluency (1-2, to be built out as volume justifies)** — visual design, page production against the page builder, component composition
- **Content operations (1, contracted or in-house)** — migration, editorial workflows, QA, post-launch content support

### Agentic tooling underneath

We use agentic development tooling across the delivery stack — for code generation, migration work, QA automation, content operations, and documentation. This is not a marketing claim and we do not sell it to clients as a feature. It is internal infrastructure that makes the blueprint model work at the cost profile it does.

The effect is observable in the numbers: 140 developer hours for 60% of a 90k engagement is only possible because the developers are operating with significant agentic leverage on the pattern-heavy work. The senior judgment — architecture, review, client-facing decisions — remains fully human. What agentic tooling replaces is the volume of repetitive implementation work that traditionally consumed the majority of developer hours.

This matters internally because it's why the margin math works. It matters externally only as an ingredient, not an identity.

### The 72-hour rhythm

We work in 72-hour internal cycles: design iterates, development builds, senior review happens, client touchpoint occurs. Three days, repeating.

This is not a secret methodology. It's a visible commitment the client sees in the proposal:

- **Every 72 hours:** client receives an update — progress, decisions needed, blockers, next cycle plan
- **Every sprint (2 weeks):** formal review gate on a specific deliverable (design system, page builder, integration, etc.)
- **Three to four total approval gates** across the full engagement, not fifteen

The rhythm works because it respects both sides. Our team gets the short internal cadence it needs to iterate and correct early. The client gets predictable touchpoints without being asked to review every micro-decision.

Selling the rhythm explicitly matters. Most clients have been burned by agency engagements where they hear nothing for two weeks and then get asked to approve 40 screens at once. Our rhythm is designed to make that failure mode impossible.

### Quality gates that are real, not performative

Three formal approval gates across a Platform engagement:

**Gate 1 — System approval (end of Sprint 2).** Client signs off on the design system and page builder. After this gate, the system is locked. Changes trigger scope discussions, not rework.

**Gate 2 — Production sample review (middle of engagement).** Client reviews 5-10 production pages built on the system. Validates that the system is being applied correctly before the full production run.

**Gate 3 — Pre-launch review (end of engagement).** Full platform review, final adjustments, launch sign-off.

Between gates, clients see work continuously through the 72-hour rhythm but are not asked for formal approvals. This prevents the two worst patterns: the client who wants to approve every change (which kills velocity) and the client who disappears and then rejects everything at the end (which kills margin).

### Discovery as a qualification filter

Every Veren engagement begins with a paid discovery — a week for Platform, 2-3 days for Foundation, 1 day for Starter. Discovery is priced separately and is non-refundable.

Discovery produces: locked scope, preconditions checklist, risk register, technical architecture brief, and an honest assessment of whether Veren is the right fit for this client.

Discovery is also where we qualify the client's velocity. Specific questions we need answers to:

- Who has final approval authority? Are they available every 72 hours?
- What's legal/compliance review turnaround?
- Is there an executive sponsor who can unblock cross-functional issues?
- Who owns content readiness, and what state is content in?
- What's the realistic preconditions timeline (access, approvals, integrations)?

If the answers suggest the client can't operate at our cadence, we say so. Discovery is either the start of a successful engagement or the start of an honest "we're not the right fit" conversation. Both outcomes are valuable; neither is a failure.

### The delivery clock starts when preconditions are met

Scope, timeline, and price commitments are written into the contract. So is this: the clock starts when the client-side preconditions are complete, not when the contract is signed.

This protects both sides. Our commitment is to deliver against their readiness. Their commitment is to be ready. If their side slips, the timeline extends at no additional cost up to a defined limit, after which we rescope together.

This is the mechanism that makes fixed-timeline commitments sustainable. Without it, fixed timelines become unpaid overtime for the agency; with it, they become a shared discipline.

### Handover is part of the delivery, not an afterthought

Every engagement ends with:

- Documented component library and page builder usage
- Recorded training sessions for the client's editorial and technical teams
- A 2-week stabilization window post-launch during which we respond to issues
- Optional — genuinely optional — retainer for ongoing support

Clients who want to walk away after handover walk away, platform intact. This is deliberate. Agencies that structure their business around client dependency optimize for short-term retention at the cost of trust. We optimize for trust, which produces longer-term relationships through choice rather than lock-in.

## What a Foundation engagement actually looks like

To make the model concrete, here is how a reference Foundation engagement (80k EUR, 8-10 weeks) runs from kickoff to handover. Platform engagements scale this up; Starter engagements compress it down.

**Week 0 — Discovery (paid separately, 12-15k EUR).** Two to three days of structured workshops. Stakeholder mapping, commerce or lead-generation workshop, content and brand session, technical architecture session, synthesis. Output: locked scope, preconditions checklist, architecture brief, risk register. Client signs for the main engagement or walks. If walks, they keep the deliverables.

**Week 1 — Preconditions and kickoff.** Clock starts when preconditions checklist is complete (access granted, decision-maker confirmed, content readiness assessed, integrations scoped). Architect-led kickoff aligns the client team on rhythm, gates, and communication.

**Weeks 2-3 — Foundation build.** Design system customized from blueprint. Page builder configured for this client's page types. Content model defined. Integration architecture implemented. 3-5 reference pages built. Developer hours concentrated here, running against the blueprint rather than from scratch. End of Sprint 2: **Gate 1 — system approval.** Client signs off; system is now locked.

**Weeks 4-5 — Production ramp.** Remaining page types designed using the locked system. Developers build integration logic and system extensions. Designers move into production mode, producing pages on the page builder. Content operations begin migration preparation. Middle of Sprint 4: **Gate 2 — production sample review.**

**Weeks 6-7 — Production run.** Bulk of pages produced by designers + content-ops with minimal developer re-engagement. Any system gaps identified become small dev sprints, not large rework. QA runs continuously, not at the end. By end of Sprint 7, platform is substantially complete.

**Week 8 — Pre-launch.** **Gate 3 — pre-launch review.** Final content loads, SEO checks, performance testing, accessibility validation, launch readiness. Training sessions for client's editorial team. Documentation finalized.

**Week 9 — Launch + stabilization.** Platform goes live. One-week active monitoring, rapid response to any issues. Retainer conversation — optional, not pushed.

**Week 10 — Handover complete.** Final handover package delivered: documentation, training recordings, component library reference, blueprint adaptations specific to their platform. Client's team demonstrably operating the platform independently. Engagement formally closes.

This is not an aspirational timeline. This is the rhythm the model is built to produce.

## The three tiers

The tiers are applications of the same model at different scales, not different methodologies. Same blueprint, same rhythm, same gate structure, same handover commitment — just scoped to the client's size and needs.

### Tier 1 — Platform (CHF 130-170k, 12-14 weeks)

**For:** Mid-market companies (15-150M revenue) launching or replatforming a significant digital presence. 15-30 pages, real e-commerce or complex integrations, multi-language, multi-stakeholder organizations with at least one executive sponsor who can move at our cadence.

**Includes:** Full discovery week, design system, Sanity implementation, page builder with custom components, e-commerce integration (Shopify or equivalent), content migration at a committed rate, analytics setup, team training, 2 weeks post-launch stabilization.

**Two clocks:** Foundation typically 6-8 weeks, Production 4-6 weeks running in parallel from Sprint 3 onward.

**The buyer:** Head of Digital, CMO, or in some cases CEO. Someone with budget authority and direct accountability for the launch.

### Tier 2 — Foundation (CHF 70-95k, 8-10 weeks)

**For:** Ambitious SMBs and smaller mid-market companies (5-30M revenue) building their first serious digital platform or replatforming from something underserving them. 8-12 pages, standard commerce or lead generation, single or dual language.

**Includes:** Condensed discovery (2-3 days), design system derived directly from blueprint with moderate customization, Sanity implementation, page builder, standard integrations, content migration, training, 1 week post-launch support.

**Two clocks:** Foundation typically 4-5 weeks, Production 3-4 weeks running in parallel from Sprint 2 onward.

**The buyer:** Founder, CEO, or Head of Marketing. Fewer layers of approval, faster decisions, tighter budgets — but also clearer ownership and faster velocity on their side.

### Tier 3 — Starter (CHF 25-40k, 4-5 weeks)

**For:** Early-stage companies, B2B startups post-seed, spinouts from larger organizations, or established SMBs with simple digital needs. 5-7 pages, content-focused, standard forms and lead capture, single language.

**Includes:** 1-day discovery, blueprint-directed design direction with light customization, Sanity setup on blueprint, limited bespoke work, content population with client, launch.

**Two clocks:** Foundation compressed to 2-3 weeks, Production 1-2 weeks. Minimal parallelization at this scale.

**The buyer:** Founder or marketing lead. Often technically savvy enough to operate the platform themselves post-launch with minimal training.

**Why Starter exists:** It's our market-entry product and our conversion funnel. Clients who start here often grow into Foundation or Platform engagements in year 2. It's also our case-study generator — we can deliver several Starter engagements in the time a single Platform takes, which builds our portfolio and reputation faster. Margin per project is lower; lifetime value and strategic value are high.

## The commercial math (internal only, honest version)

This section is for Victor and Merdan to understand the actual economics of the firm. Numbers are approximate and should be refined with real data over the next 2-3 engagements.

### Cost structure

**Fixed monthly cost (current):**
- Developer team: 200 hours/month × 30 EUR = 6,000 EUR/month
- Infrastructure, tooling, Sanity licenses, agentic tooling subscriptions: ~1,500 EUR/month
- Total fixed operational cost: ~7,500 EUR/month

**Variable per-engagement cost:**
- Victor architect time: ~40-80 hours per engagement depending on tier
- Merdan commercial time: ~20-40 hours per engagement (sales, relationship, coordination)
- Designer time (currently contracted, moving toward in-house): ~40-100 hours per engagement
- Developer hours drawn from monthly allocation

### Reference engagement: Foundation at 80k EUR

- Revenue: 80,000 EUR
- Direct developer cost: ~200 hours × 30 EUR = 6,000 EUR (extrapolating from 140h/60% reference)
- Designer cost (contracted): ~80 hours × 60 EUR = 4,800 EUR
- Infrastructure amortization (portion of monthly): ~3,000 EUR over 8-10 weeks
- Total direct delivery cost: ~13,800 EUR
- Available for Victor + Merdan compensation + firm margin: ~66,200 EUR
- If Victor + Merdan take 40,000 EUR combined for this engagement, firm margin is ~26,000 EUR (~32% of revenue)

This is an unusual margin profile for an agency-style business. It exists because the developer-hour-to-revenue ratio is dramatically below agency norms, which is a direct consequence of the blueprint model and agentic tooling.

### Reference engagement: Platform at 150k EUR

- Revenue: 150,000 EUR
- Direct developer cost: ~400 hours × 30 EUR = 12,000 EUR
- Designer cost: ~150 hours × 60 EUR = 9,000 EUR
- Content operations: ~80 hours × 40 EUR = 3,200 EUR
- Infrastructure amortization: ~5,000 EUR
- Total direct delivery cost: ~29,200 EUR
- Available for Victor + Merdan compensation + firm margin: ~120,800 EUR
- If Victor + Merdan take 75,000 EUR combined, firm margin is ~45,800 EUR (~30% of revenue)

### What this means

Margin at 30-35% of revenue is healthy for this kind of business. The numbers work because our delivery model is structurally light on the most expensive resource (developer hours) and heavy on the resource we already have (senior judgment from Victor and Merdan).

The decision about internalizing the current freelance developer team is not a cost decision — the economics already work at freelance rates. It's a decision about reliability, control, and availability as engagement volume grows. Internalizing becomes worth it when we have consistent multi-engagement volume that justifies full-time commitment from the team, which is probably 6-12 months out.

The real constraint on growth is not developer capacity. It is Victor's architect hours and Merdan's commercial hours. Growth decisions are therefore about when to duplicate the *senior* layer — eventually hiring a second architect or adding a commercial partner — not about scaling the delivery layer.

### What this does NOT mean

These numbers are not a guarantee of margin on every engagement. They represent what a well-run engagement looks like. Engagements that slip timelines, suffer scope creep, or hit unexpected integration complexity can easily consume 2-3x the baseline hours and collapse margin. This is why discovery qualification, preconditions enforcement, and gate discipline are not optional — they are the mechanisms that protect the economics.

## The agency channel

Veren operates two sales channels. The direct channel sells platforms to end clients. The partner channel sells delivery capacity to other agencies.

### Why the partner channel exists

Our most proven revenue path has been through BEE — 830k EUR generated in six months by letting BEE upsell our platform delivery into their HubSpot client base. This is not a theoretical model. It is our actual operational history.

The logic is straightforward: HubSpot agencies have client relationships we cannot efficiently build ourselves. Their clients frequently need platform work the agency cannot efficiently deliver itself (CMS Hub limitations, design-heavy work, e-commerce complexity, multi-language requirements). We close that gap. The agency captures project revenue they would otherwise leave on the table or lose to a competitor; we execute delivery at the cost profile our model enables; the client gets the platform they need.

### Target partner profile

The ideal partner agency has: a HubSpot specialization, a base of 20+ mid-market clients, a commercial team that can identify platform-expansion opportunities, and the organizational maturity to work with an external delivery partner without defensiveness. In DACH, we estimate there are 30-50 agencies that fit this profile, of which we only need 3-5 active partnerships to saturate our delivery capacity.

### The partner pitch

*"One of our current partners generated 830k EUR in platform revenue over six months by letting us handle web platform work their clients were asking for. They kept the client relationship, the account expansion, and the recurring HubSpot work. We delivered the platform pieces they couldn't efficiently build themselves. You have similar clients. Let's talk about the same arrangement."*

This is a concrete, evidenced pitch. It lands differently than a capability-sell because it references real revenue generated for a real partner.

### Commercial structure with partners

Several structures are possible depending on the partner:

- **White-label delivery.** Partner sells under their brand, we deliver invisibly. Pricing typically at 60-70% of partner's client-facing rate.
- **Co-branded delivery.** "Agency + Veren" framing. Pricing typically at 70-80% of partner's rate.
- **Referral + direct delivery.** Partner refers, we contract directly with the end client, partner takes a referral commission (10-20%).

Which structure applies depends on the partner's preference, the client's preference, and the strategic value of the relationship. The document does not prescribe one; it acknowledges all three are viable.

### Boundary with BEE specifically

BEE is our most important current partner. The terms above describe how Veren operates with partner agencies generally; the specific arrangement with BEE requires a dedicated conversation that has not yet happened (see BEE section below). Until that conversation, we continue operating with BEE under current terms and do not pursue other HubSpot agency partnerships that would conflict commercially.

## The Stratum path

Sanity is where we start. It's the right tool for 90% of what our clients need on day one: fast content operations, strong editorial experience, proven scale, low total cost of ownership. We're experts in it, we have the blueprint, we can deliver on it at the velocity we promise.

Stratum is where we go when clients need more than Sanity can deliver on its own. Specifically: deep multi-tenant architecture, complex operational workflows, custom data models that go beyond content, GraphQL-native integrations, RLS-enforced security for regulated industries. Stratum is our own schema-driven backend platform, built in NestJS with PostgreSQL, designed to sit behind or alongside content platforms rather than replace them.

For the next 12 months, Stratum is in pilot with a Romanian client (FNT, via Prodeum Aseris). That pilot is where Stratum earns its production credibility. We are deliberately not selling Stratum externally during this period. When the pilot concludes and we have production scars to point to, Stratum becomes a real offering for Veren clients who've outgrown their Sanity implementation or whose needs from day one exceed what Sanity was designed for.

The commercial logic: Sanity is our door opener and our most-common delivery. Stratum is our IP and our long-term margin expander. We lead with Sanity because buyers trust it. We introduce Stratum when it's the right answer, not as a bait-and-switch.

This sequencing matters for honesty. Clients who engage Veren for a Sanity platform are not secretly being prepared for a Stratum upsell. They get a fully working, independent, handed-over Sanity platform. Stratum is a future option, not a hidden dependency.

BEE is our most important commercial relationship today. Whatever Veren becomes, BEE's success continues to matter to us commercially and personally.

### Principles we operate by

These are our red lines, regardless of how the Veren transition unfolds:

1. **We continue to deliver current BEE engagements to our full standard.** No reduction in quality, attention, or commitment as Veren develops. Current work comes first.
2. **We do not approach BEE's existing clients directly for Veren work without BEE's knowledge and agreement.** Clients who came through BEE are BEE clients. Ambiguity here damages trust and probably contracts.

4. **We do not poach BEE team members for Veren.** The three developers we work with, the designers and coordinators we interact with — not recruitment targets.
5. **We do not undermine BEE's commercial positioning when talking to shared prospects.** If a prospect came to us through BEE, the conversation stays through BEE. If a prospect approaches us independently, we are honest about the relationship landscape.
6. **We are transparent with BEE about Veren's existence and direction once this foundation document is locked.** The conversation happens; it does not happen before we know what we are saying, and it does not not-happen because it's uncomfortable.

### The conversation we need to have with BEE

Timing: once this foundation document is stable between Victor and Merdan and we have clarity on what Veren is, we open the conversation with BEE leadership. Probably within 60 days of finalizing this document.

Framing: not a negotiation, not a confrontation. A transparent sharing of what we are building and an invitation to define the future shape of the BEE-Veren relationship together. Possible outcomes range from "continue current arrangement indefinitely" to "formalize a partnership agreement where Veren is BEE's strategic delivery partner" to "phase down the engagement on mutually agreed terms."

The worst outcome is BEE learning about Veren from someone else, or discovering it on LinkedIn before we've told them. That outcome we prevent at all costs, which is why this conversation is a real priority once the foundation is in place.

### What we gain by handling this well

A transparent, well-handled transition with BEE produces: a formalized partner agreement (likely more commercially favorable than the current hourly structure), BEE as a named reference partner for Veren, continued revenue flow through the BEE channel even as direct-sales channels mature, and most importantly, a professional relationship that remains intact.

A poorly-handled transition produces: damaged trust, potential contract disputes, reputation risk in the DACH agency ecosystem (which is small and talks), loss of the 830k EUR/6-month momentum, and personal cost to people who have treated us well.

This is not just a commercial calculation. It's an integrity question. We want to be the kind of firm that people trust specifically because we handle relationships like this well, even when it would be easier not to.

## How we work — first principles

1. **We don't sell hours.** We sell scope and timeline. When we're faster, that's our margin — earned through investment in blueprint and methodology. When we're slower, that's our loss. Clients know what they're paying for and what they're getting.

2. **We don't take clients we can't help.** Discovery is a qualification filter as much as a scoping tool. If a prospect can't move at our cadence, can't commit the preconditions, or is looking for a different kind of firm, we say so — early and honestly. The wrong client is worse than no client.

3. **We commit in writing.** Scope, timeline, quality, price. We absorb the risk of our own delivery. Clients absorb the risk of their own preconditions. Delays caused by their side extend the timeline without penalty up to a defined limit; delays caused by ours are on us.

4. **We leave clients independent.** Every engagement ends with documentation, training, and genuine operational handover. Ongoing retainers are optional, not structural. We'd rather have clients who choose to come back than clients who have to.

5. **We're honest about what we don't do.** We don't do brand identity from scratch, paid media, or enterprise IT integration beyond our scope. We partner for those when clients need them. Clarity about our edges is part of our value.

6. **We handle partnerships with integrity.** Partner agencies are not channels to exploit. They are relationships to invest in. We keep our commitments to them even when it would be commercially easier not to, because the alternative — a reputation for being untrustworthy with partners — would destroy the firm faster than any market condition.

7. **We protect the firm.** We don't chase bad work for short-term revenue. We don't over-promise to win bids. We don't burn the team. A good firm in 5 years requires good decisions today, especially the unglamorous ones.

**A good 12 months looks like:**

- BEE relationship damaged because the transparency conversation was delayed or mishandled

- Team overworked on too many parallel engagements because capacity wasn't managed honestly

- Founders diverging on direction because foundation wasn't pressure-tested early enough

The gap between those two outcomes isn't mostly about market luck. It's about whether we execute three things in parallel: continued excellent delivery on current BEE engagements, active development of the independent Veren channels (direct + new partners), and honest handling of the BEE transition. Any one of those three we can do. All three simultaneously is the real work.

## Open questions

These are the things we haven't answered yet. Naming them explicitly is more useful than pretending they're settled.

1. **The name.** Veren is a placeholder. It may be the name; it may not. We need to decide by end of month so the rest of the work has something to anchor to.

2. **Legal structure.** Cyprus and Romanian entities planned. Do we also need a German GmbH or Swiss AG presence for DACH credibility at Tier 1 price points? What's the cost/benefit tradeoff, and at what revenue threshold does it become worth the complexity?

3. **The BEE conversation — timing and framing.** We've agreed it happens within 60 days of finalizing this document. Who opens it, what's the specific framing, what's our ask? A short prep session before the conversation is probably worth an hour of our time.

4. **The content production rate.** Our model rests on a committed pages-per-sprint rate during Production. We need to set that rate based on real team velocity, not theory. Next live project is the measurement opportunity.

5. **Tier 3 market entry.** We agreed Starter is our entry tier and conversion funnel. But we haven't yet identified the specific channels to reach Starter buyers. Is it content-led? Partnership-led? Network-led? This shapes Merdan's first sales priorities.

6. **First direct client.** We need one Platform or Foundation engagement sourced outside BEE within 6 months. Who's on the target list? Which of our networks do we activate? This needs to become concrete.

7. **Partner agency outreach sequence.** Which 3-5 HubSpot agencies in DACH are the priority targets for the partner pitch? When do we start that outreach, and does it happen before or after the BEE conversation? (The honest answer is probably: after, because approaching other HubSpot agencies before BEE knows about Veren creates an avoidable integrity problem.)

8. **Team internalization decision.** When and how we move the current freelance developer team toward full-time engagement. Probably driven by consistent multi-engagement volume over 2-3 quarters, not by cost.

9. **Designer and content-ops hires.** The model says these are the next capacity investments, ahead of more developers. Who, when, and at what engagement volume trigger?

10. **Stratum commercial framing.** When the FNT pilot concludes, how do we talk about Stratum publicly? As Veren's platform? As a separate product? As both? Affects positioning and has second-order effects on firm identity.
