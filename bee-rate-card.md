## Purpose

This document defines our commercial framework for working with BEE going forward. It covers our rate card across roles, the two engagement models we operate under (client-facing co-delivery and internal BEE work), and the principles that guide how we collaborate.

It is meant as a working reference between partners — not a contract. Specific projects continue to be scoped and agreed individually, with this framework as the underlying structure.

---

## How we work together

Our relationship with BEE operates across two distinct engagement models. The model determines pricing, ownership, and how the work is positioned to the end client.

**Model 1 — Client-facing co-delivery.** BEE sells a project to a client; we co-deliver. The client may see us as part of BEE's extended team, or as a named delivery partner depending on what the relationship requires. Pricing for these engagements uses our **external rate card** below, or — for fixed-budget projects — a **revenue split** between BEE and Veren scaled to project size.

**Model 2 — Internal BEE work.** BEE engages us directly for internal projects, support work, or BEE's own commercial initiatives where there is no end client billing relationship. Pricing for these engagements uses our **internal rate card**, which reflects a 40% partner discount across all roles.

The distinction is important because it protects both sides. External work is priced to be competitive in BEE's market and to allow BEE healthy margin on top. Internal work is priced as a partner gesture that reflects the strategic value of the BEE relationship.

## Co-sold projects with revenue split

For fixed-budget client projects where BEE and Veren co-sell and co-deliver as joint partners, we use a revenue-split model rather than hourly billing. The split is calculated on the total project budget and scales with project size:

| Total project budget | Veren share | BEE share |
|----------------------|-------------|-----------|
| Below 150,000 EUR | 60% | 40% |
| 150,000 – 500,000 EUR | 65% | 35% |
| 500,000 EUR and above | 70% | 30% |

**The split is applied to the entire project budget at the rate corresponding to the budget tier** — not cumulatively across tiers. A 200,000 EUR project is split 65/35 across the full amount; a 600,000 EUR project is split 70/30 across the full amount.

**What each side covers under this model:**

*Veren responsibility:*
- All delivery: architecture, development, design, QA, content operations, project management on the delivery side
- Technical decisions, quality assurance, deployment
- Direct client communication on technical and delivery topics

*BEE responsibility:*
- Lead generation and sales
- Account management and the primary client relationship
- Strategic positioning and proposal development
- Commercial terms, contracting, invoicing the end client
- Account expansion and relationship continuity post-launch

**Why this scales with budget size:** Larger projects carry more delivery complexity and more delivery risk, both of which Veren absorbs. The increasing share at higher tiers reflects that cost and risk distribution. It also reflects the reality that on larger engagements, the proportional value of sales and account management decreases relative to the value of execution capacity.

**Out-of-scope additions** (changes requested after initial scope is agreed) are handled separately at external hourly rates, with the same split applied to the change-order revenue.

## How we operate together

Beyond rates and splits, a few operating principles that have made the working relationship effective so far and that we want to continue:

**1. Clear delivery ownership on our side.** Within an engagement, Veren takes operational ownership of delivery — the team, the timeline, the quality. BEE provides client context, strategic direction, and account-level guidance. This division has worked because both sides respect the other's primary domain.

**2. Single point of contact per project.** On Veren's side, one of us (Victor or Merdan, depending on the project) is the named lead for each engagement. On BEE's side, the same: one named account owner. This avoids the multi-thread confusion that creeps in when partner agencies work together at scale.

**3. Transparent capacity planning.** We tell BEE honestly what our delivery capacity looks like for the coming 4-8 weeks. BEE tells us what's in the pipeline that may convert. We plan together rather than reactively. When we cannot take on additional work, we say so early enough for BEE to plan around it.

**4. Honest scope conversations.** When a project's scope is unclear or unrealistic for the budget, we say so during scoping rather than after starting delivery. If a project starts to drift in execution, we raise it as soon as it's visible. The principle is: partners surface problems, they don't hide them.

**5. Shared protection of the end client.** Both sides represent BEE to the end client. We do not contradict each other in front of clients, do not raise commercial issues with them that should be handled between BEE and us, and do not undermine each other's positioning. Disagreements are resolved between BEE and Veren, not in front of the client.

**6. Discretion on commercial terms.** Rates, splits, and the specific commercial structure of our partnership are not discussed with end clients or third parties. The end client buys from BEE; the BEE-Veren commercial structure is internal to the partnership.

## Practical operating notes

**Estimation and quoting.** When BEE needs an estimate for a prospect, we provide it within agreed turnaround times (typically 2-3 working days for standard scope, longer for complex engagements). Estimates are firm for 30 days unless scope changes materially.

**Invoicing rhythm.** For T&M engagements, we invoice monthly based on logged hours. For revenue-split engagements, invoicing follows the milestone schedule agreed for the project, with our share calculated and invoiced against each milestone.

**Engagement onboarding.** New projects begin with a brief alignment session between the named leads on both sides — scope, timeline, communication cadence, escalation path. Typically 30-60 minutes; not a workshop, an alignment.

**Team allocation.** Veren commits team members to projects based on the agreed scope. Substitutions, if needed mid-project, are discussed with BEE in advance.

**Currency and contracting.** All amounts in this document are EUR. Veren operates through Prodeum Aseris (Romania) for invoicing purposes; specific contracting structures can be discussed per engagement based on what works best for BEE and the end client.

## What this document is, and isn't

This rate card and framework is a working reference. It is not a contract — specific engagements are still scoped, quoted, and agreed individually. It is not exhaustive — situations not covered here are handled by conversation, not by document interpretation.

What it is: a clear, honest baseline that both sides can refer to when planning new work, scoping engagements, or setting client expectations. The goal is to remove ambiguity from the commercial layer so we can both focus on the delivery and growth that actually matter.

## Open items for discussion

A few things this document doesn't yet settle that we'd like to discuss together:

1. **Whether we want a written partnership agreement** beyond this framework — covering things like exclusivity (or non-exclusivity), IP ownership for blueprint and tooling, dispute resolution, and partnership termination terms. This document does not require that, but it may be the right next step depending on how BEE wants to formalize the relationship.

2. **Forward planning visibility.** How far ahead do we want to plan capacity together? Monthly? Quarterly? More structured pipeline reviews would help both sides allocate better.

3. **Reference and case study rights.** As both BEE and Veren build their respective profiles, how we handle case studies, references, and naming each other in marketing is worth agreeing explicitly. Currently handled case-by-case; could benefit from a baseline understanding.

4. **Scaling scenarios.** If volume grows beyond current capacity — either from BEE's pipeline or Veren's other channels — how do we adjust the partnership structure? Better to discuss before the situation arises than during.

These are not blockers. The current arrangement works. These are conversations for when there's space — probably over the next month or two.
